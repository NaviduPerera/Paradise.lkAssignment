Yala National Park

Sithulpauwwa

The name Sithulpauwwa is derived from the word 'Chittalapabbata' which means the hill of the quiet mind. The ancient rock temple is a place of worship for devotees. It is believed that in ancient times this rock temple housed a total of 12,000 monks.

Bird Watching

The number of water birds inhabiting the wetlands of Yala is around 90, and roughly half of them are migrants. Other water birds include Lesser Flamingo and pelicans, as well as rare species such as Purple Heron, Night herons, Egrets, Purple Swamphen and the Oriental Darter. Forest birds are Orange-breasted Green Pigeon, Hornbills, Old World flycatchers, Asian Paradise-flycatcher, Asian barbets, and Orioles. Crested Serpent-eagle and White-bellied Sea Eagle are the raptors of the park.

Magul Maha Viharaya

Located to the south of Sithulpauwa, Magul Maha Viharaya is believed to have provided the setting for the marriage of King KavanTissa to Vihara Maha Devi. One could combine a visit to Sithulpauwe and Magul MahaViharaya as they are located closely together.

Wilpattu National Park

Jeep safaris are the usual when visiting Wilpattu National Park. While leopards, elephants, sloth bears, deer, and endemic birds can all be seen on a half-day safari, a full-day safari at a National Park provides more opportunities to interact with animals. Tourists are not allowed to get out of the jeep when they come across the tracks of wild animals, as this exposes them to probable encounters in the wilderness. Visitors to the Wilpattu National Park are always safe, even when they are in the middle of nowhere, thanks to the rangers who guide the wildlife safaris. The park has two entrances: (A) Hunuwilgam Entrance and (B) Eluwankulama Entrance.
